Urban Heat Island Foundation Model

Towards an interpretable spatio-temporal foundation model for UHI forecasting, causal attribution, and climate adaptation — jointly modelling LST-UHI and AT-UHI across cities.

UHIFM Research Roadmap — Three-Phase Development Plan
1 / 1
Research Vision

This PhD project investigates whether a unified, physically informed, and interpretable spatio-temporal foundation model can support transferable UHI forecasting and attribution across cities. The model is pre-trained on heterogeneous multi-city UHI datasets and adapted to forecasting, interpolation, attribution, and scenario simulation.

Key Research Questions
  • How can LST-UHI and AT-UHI be jointly represented in a single model?
  • Which physical inductive biases improve cross-city generalisation?
  • How can uncertainty-aware outputs inform climate adaptation decisions?
  • Can one model bridge forecasting accuracy and causal attribution?

Adaptive Urban Heat Mitigation Through Ensemble Learning

AutoML ensemble modeling and scenario simulation for equitable UHI mitigation in Vancouver — quantifying intervention effectiveness and distributional fairness across social vulnerability groups.

Overview

AutoGluon ensemble AutoML predicts UHI intensity across four seasonal-diurnal scenarios for 1,660 Dissemination Areas in Vancouver. Permutation-based feature importance reveals the dominant cooling role of vegetation and water proximity. Scenario simulation (0–2× feature scaling) quantifies the marginal cooling of each intervention and evaluates distributional equity across socially vulnerable population groups.

Key Contributions
  • AutoGluon ensemble (CatBoost, NN-Torch, XT) with bagging and multi-layer stacking — R² up to 0.79
  • Permutation-based feature importance across 4 seasonal-diurnal UHI scenarios
  • 0–2× feature scaling simulation isolating marginal cooling contribution per intervention
  • Vegetation cover delivers strongest cooling (up to 2.95 °C) and consistently advances equity
  • Built-form reductions (BCR, road density) improve distributional justice with limited thermal compromise

Multimodal Urban Heat Sensing: AutoML + Geotagged Social Media

Fusing satellite-derived UHI modeling with geotagged Twitter/X VADER sentiment analysis — bridging objective thermal exposure and subjective heat perception to surface climate justice inequities in Metro Vancouver.

Congress Presentation — 61st ISOCARP World Planning Congress, Riyadh
Metro Vancouver — Study Area and UHI Spatial Patterns
Heat Exposure and Socio-Demographic Vulnerability Mapping
Ensemble Model — Feature Importance Analysis
Intervention Scenario Comparison
Climate Justice — Equity-Efficiency Trade-off Analysis
Policy Recommendations for Equitable Heat Mitigation
1 / 7
Overview

A multimodal framework that fuses two complementary data streams: (1) satellite-derived UHI intensity modeled via AutoGluon ensemble AutoML across four seasonal-diurnal scenarios, and (2) geotagged Twitter/X posts processed through VADER sentiment analysis to construct a spatially referenced Perceived Heat Score. Overlaying both layers across 1,660 Dissemination Areas in Metro Vancouver surfaces perception–exposure mismatches — communities with high modeled UHI but low social media signal, potentially the most underserved in climate intervention — and links differential heat burden to race, income, education, and citizenship dimensions of climate justice.

Key Contributions
  • Multimodal fusion: satellite UHI (AutoGluon AutoML) × geotagged Twitter/X (VADER sentiment) — two independent heat signals spatially co-registered at DA level
  • Perceived Heat Score constructed from filtered heat-keyword tweets, scored for sentiment intensity and mapped via KDE
  • Perception–exposure mismatch analysis: high UHI / low tweet signal identifies silenced communities at disproportionate risk
  • AutoGluon ensemble across 4 seasonal-diurnal scenarios, R² up to 0.79; permutation importance reveals vegetation and water proximity as dominant cooling factors
  • Climate justice: racialized, low-income, non-citizen, and low-education communities show systematically higher UHI exposure across all scenarios
